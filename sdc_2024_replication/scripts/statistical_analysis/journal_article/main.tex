\begin{abstract}
International migration is numerically small in many U.S.\ states yet demographically decisive in the periphery, where low fertility, population aging, and domestic out-migration leave migration as the primary swing factor in population change. Forecasting these flows is unusually difficult because arrivals are often ``lumpy''---shaped by discrete refugee resettlement cohorts and employer-specific recruitment---and because key levers are federal policy and local resettlement capacity rather than stable long-run trends. This study develops and applies a multi-method empirical framework for forecasting net international migration to North Dakota, a state of roughly 797,000 residents that receives about 0.17\% of national international migration despite holding about 0.23\% of the U.S.\ population.

Using the Census Bureau Population Estimates Program (\PEP) net international migration component as the forecasting target (2010--2024) and triangulating with Refugee Processing Center arrivals (FY2002--FY2024), DHS lawful permanent resident admissions, and \ACS{} foreign-born stocks, we combine descriptive concentration measures, time-series diagnostics, cross-state benchmarking, gravity-style allocation models, machine-learning diagnostics, quasi-experimental policy evaluation, wave-duration modeling, and Monte Carlo scenario simulation.

Results show that North Dakota's migration profile is refugee-dominated and origin-specific: location quotients exceed 40 for Liberia and exceed 20 for several other African origins. Annual net international migration is highly volatile (coefficient of variation 82.5\%) and the series is approximately integrated, implying that departures from historical averages can persist rather than rapidly mean-revert. Structural break analysis identifies major regime shifts in 2020 and 2021 associated with the COVID-19 pandemic ($F = 16.01$, $p < 0.001$). Aggregate break tests detect no statistically significant 2017 shift, yet nationality-level difference-in-differences indicates that refugee arrivals from Travel Ban-affected origins declined by roughly 75\% relative to unaffected origins (conventional $p = 0.031$), illustrating how policy effects can be compositional and invisible in state totals. Because parallel-trends diagnostics are mixed and inference is sensitive with only seven treated nationalities, this estimate is interpreted as descriptive evidence of policy sensitivity rather than a precise causal effect magnitude. Cross-sectional allocation estimates suggest diaspora associations attenuate sharply once population mass is controlled.

Scenario projections through 2045 yield a median net international migration of 9,119 with a conservative 95\% uncertainty envelope of 3,407--14,806. These results argue for scenario-based planning and transparent uncertainty quantification when forecasting migration in small, policy-sensitive states.
\end{abstract}
