## Executive Summary

North Dakota's population is projected under a single public baseline path to
help public agencies, communities, and residents plan for future needs. The
Baseline (CBO-Adjusted) scenario applies the CBO additive migration adjustment
and a -5% fertility adjustment.

The unadjusted trend-continuation path remains an internal sensitivity.
Restricted Growth and High Growth are legacy/internal sensitivities and are not
part of the public release.

The baseline indicates continued growth in some parts of the state, decline in
others, and a shift toward older age groups. Growth is concentrated in a
smaller set of counties, so statewide change does not mean every local area
grows at the same pace.

Suggested exhibit: key-number callout with 2025 population and 2055 baseline
population.

## How To Read These Projections

These are scenario projections, but the public package presents only the
Baseline (CBO-Adjusted) path. Use it as a planning path, not as a guaranteed
future.

The baseline reflects the CBO additive migration adjustment and the -5%
fertility adjustment. Internal sensitivity paths remain available for model
testing, but they are not part of the public marketing package.

Because the baseline is an assumption-based projection, the numbers should be
used as planning tools rather than as a single fixed outcome. The point is not
certainty; the point is to show how the population path behaves under the
public baseline assumptions.

Suggested exhibit: one-row baseline assumptions box.

## Statewide Population Outlook

In the public baseline, North Dakota grows across the projection horizon.
Growth is strongest when migration and fertility assumptions support it, and
slower when those assumptions soften.

This is a projection path, not a guaranteed future. It shows what would happen
if the Baseline (CBO-Adjusted) assumptions hold over time.

Suggested exhibit: 2025-2055 baseline line chart.

## Regional Population Change

Regional patterns differ in the baseline. Some regions add population, while
others decline or remain relatively flat.

These regional differences show why statewide totals need local context. A
statewide increase can occur while some regions experience population decline.

Suggested exhibit: region map paired with a baseline regional change bar chart.

## County Growth And Concentration

County growth is concentrated in the baseline. A smaller group of counties
accounts for most gross growth, while other counties decline.

The county pattern is one of the most important messages for public users: the
statewide baseline total does not describe the experience of every county.

Suggested exhibit: top-growth and largest-decline county bars, or a county map
with a compact ranked table.

## Age Structure

The baseline shows different patterns by age group. Working-age residents and
residents age 65 and older grow more quickly than the under-18 population.

These shifts have practical implications. A larger older population affects
health care, long-term care, transportation, housing, and community services.
Working-age growth affects labor supply and economic planning. A comparatively
flat under-18 population affects school and family-service planning differently
across counties.

Suggested exhibit: age-group trend chart with under 18, 18-64, 65+, and 85+.

## Methodology Summary

The projections use a cohort-component method. The model begins with a base
population by age, sex, race and ethnicity, and geography, then advances the
population year by year using fertility, mortality, and migration assumptions.

The public baseline uses Census Population Estimates Program data, North Dakota
and national fertility inputs, survival assumptions, and migration rates with
the CBO additive migration adjustment and the -5% fertility adjustment. State
totals are built from county projections so that county, regional, and state
outputs remain internally consistent.

## Cautions And Data Availability

Long-term population projections are sensitive to migration. North Dakota's
recent growth has depended heavily on migration patterns, and those patterns
can change with federal policy, global conditions, economic cycles, housing
availability, and local labor demand.

Growth is also geographically concentrated. In the baseline, a small group of
counties accounts for most gross growth, while other counties decline. Users
should review county and regional detail instead of relying only on statewide
totals.

The public download package will include one consolidated Excel workbook and
one consolidated CSV covering the state, regions, and counties. City and place
projections are not included in the public release.
