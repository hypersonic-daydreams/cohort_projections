## Executive Summary

North Dakota's population is projected under three scenarios to help public
agencies, communities, and residents plan for a range of demographic futures. In
the Baseline (Trend Continuation) scenario, the state grows from about 799,000
residents in 2025 to about 882,000 residents in 2055, an increase of about
83,000 residents.

That baseline should be read alongside the Restricted Growth (CBO
Policy-Adjusted) and High Growth (Elevated Immigration) scenarios. In the draft
outputs, the 2055 scenario range runs from about 828,000 residents under
Restricted Growth to about 1.08 million residents under High Growth. The range
shows how strongly long-term population change depends on migration, federal
policy, economic conditions, and demographic behavior.

Growth is not evenly distributed across the state. In the draft baseline, the
largest regional increases occur in the Fargo, Williston, and Bismarck regions.
County-level growth is concentrated in a smaller set of counties, led by Cass,
Williams, Burleigh, McKenzie, Morton, and Stark. Several regions and counties
decline in the same scenario, so statewide growth should not be read as uniform
local growth.

The age structure also changes. The draft baseline shows growth in working-age
residents and residents age 65 and older, while the under-18 population remains
comparatively flat. These shifts matter for workforce planning, schools, health
care, transportation, housing, and other public services.

population, and the 2055 scenario range.

## How To Read These Projections

These are scenario projections. Each scenario describes a different set of
demographic assumptions and shows how population would change if those
assumptions hold over time.

Baseline (Trend Continuation) extends recent demographic patterns forward.
Restricted Growth (CBO Policy-Adjusted) applies a lower migration and fertility
path grounded in Congressional Budget Office assumptions. High Growth (Elevated
Immigration) shows a higher migration and fertility path for planning context.

Because the scenarios depend on assumptions, the numbers should be used as
planning tools rather than as a single guaranteed path. The differences among
scenarios are part of the information. They show where the future is more
sensitive to migration, age structure, and geographic concentration.

## Statewide Population Outlook

In the draft baseline, North Dakota grows from about 799,000 residents in 2025
to about 882,000 residents in 2055. That is an increase of about 83,000
residents, or about 10%.

The Restricted Growth scenario grows more slowly, reaching about 828,000
residents in 2055. The High Growth scenario reaches about 1.08 million residents
in 2055. The gap among these scenarios is a central finding for public planning:
migration assumptions have a large effect on the long-term state total.

This is a trend-continuation scenario, not a guaranteed future. It shows what
would happen if recent demographic patterns continue. The companion scenarios
show how the path changes under different assumptions.

baseline visually primary while keeping Restricted Growth and High Growth
clearly visible.

## Regional Population Change

Regional patterns differ sharply in the draft baseline. The Fargo region adds
about 47,000 residents between 2025 and 2055. The Williston region adds about
36,000 residents, and the Bismarck region adds about 28,000 residents.

Other regions decline in the same scenario. The Grand Forks region declines by
about 13,000 residents, the Minot region by about 12,000 residents, the
Jamestown region by about 6,000 residents, and the Devils Lake region by about
4,000 residents. The Dickinson region grows by about 7,000 residents.

These regional differences show why statewide totals need local context. A
statewide increase can occur while some regions experience population decline.

Suggested exhibit: region map paired with a baseline regional change bar chart.

## County Growth And Concentration

County growth is concentrated in the draft baseline. Cass County adds about
51,000 residents between 2025 and 2055. Williams County adds about 24,000,
Burleigh County about 19,000, McKenzie County about 12,000, Morton County about
11,000, and Stark County about 7,000.

These gains are partly offset by declines elsewhere. Ward County declines by
about 11,000 residents, Grand Forks County by about 9,000, Walsh County by about
3,000, Pembina County by about 2,000, Stutsman County by about 2,000, and
Richland County by about 2,000.

The county pattern is one of the most important messages for public users: the
statewide baseline total does not describe the experience of every county.

with a compact ranked table.

## Age Structure

The draft baseline shows different patterns by age group. The under-18
population changes little across the projection horizon, moving from about
189,000 residents in 2025 to about 187,000 in 2055.

The working-age population, ages 18 to 64, grows from about 476,000 to about
536,000 residents. The population age 65 and older grows from about 135,000 to
about 159,000 residents. The population age 85 and older grows from about
17,000 to about 25,000 residents.

These shifts have practical implications. A larger older population affects
health care, long-term care, transportation, housing, and community services.
Working-age growth affects labor supply and economic planning. A comparatively
flat under-18 population affects school and family-service planning differently
across counties.

Suggested exhibit: age-group trend chart with under 18, 18-64, 65+, and 85+.

## Methodology Summary

The projections use a cohort-component method. The model begins with a base
population by age, sex, race and ethnicity, and geography, then advances the
population year by year using fertility, mortality, and migration assumptions.

The current system uses Census Population Estimates Program data, North Dakota
and national fertility inputs, survival assumptions, and migration rates based
on Census PEP components of change. State totals are built from county
projections so that county, regional, and state outputs remain internally
consistent.

The public scenarios are designed for planning. Baseline represents recent
trend continuation. Restricted Growth uses a CBO-grounded adjustment to reflect
lower migration and fertility assumptions. High Growth represents an elevated
migration and fertility path for comparison.

## Cautions And Data Availability

Long-term population projections are sensitive to migration. North Dakota's
recent growth has depended heavily on migration patterns, and those patterns can
change with federal policy, global conditions, economic cycles, housing
availability, and local labor demand.

Growth is also geographically concentrated. In the draft baseline, a small group
of counties accounts for most gross growth, while other counties decline. Users
should review county and regional detail instead of relying only on statewide
totals.

The public download package will include one consolidated Excel workbook and
one consolidated CSV covering the state, regions, and counties. City and place
projections are not included in the public release.
