## 1. Analysis of the Current Broken Mechanism

### 1.1 The Problem

The `high_growth` scenario applies `migration: "+15_percent"`, which multiplies all net migration rates by 1.15 inside `apply_migration_scenario()` (line 200-201 of `cohort_projections/core/migration.py`):

```python
elif scenario == "+15_percent":
    adjusted_rates[migration_col] = adjusted_rates[migration_col] * 1.15
```

This is applied to the convergence interpolation rates loaded from `data/processed/migration/convergence_rates_by_year.parquet`, which are net-negative across the state:

| Year Offset | Sum of Migration Rates | Counties with Net Out-Migration |
|-------------|------------------------|---------------------------------|
| 1           | -17.10                 | 45 of 53                        |
| 5           | -10.97                 | 43 of 53                        |
| 10          | -10.97                 | 43 of 53                        |
| 20          | -14.11                 | 47 of 53                        |

Multiplying a negative rate by 1.15 makes it more negative. The net effect over 53 counties is increased out-migration, producing **lower** population than baseline -- the exact opposite of the scenario's intent.

### 1.2 Why Multiplicative Scaling Is Fundamentally Wrong

The high_growth scenario models "counterfactual continuation of elevated post-2020 immigration trends" (ADR-037). This is an additive concept: more people arrive, regardless of whether the receiving county has positive or negative net migration. A county that loses 50 domestic migrants but gains 80 international migrants has net +30; the "elevated immigration" counterfactual adds to the +80, not to the +30.

When applied to total net migration rates (domestic + international combined), a multiplicative factor cannot distinguish between these components. It scales the domestic out-migration alongside the international in-migration, amplifying the wrong component.

The restricted_growth scenario avoids this problem through two design choices:
1. It uses `intl_share` to decompose total migration into domestic (unchanged) and international (scaled) components
2. Its factors (0.20 to 1.00) reduce international in-migration, which is directionally correct -- less immigration means lower migration, regardless of sign

For high_growth, even applying `intl_share` decomposition with a factor > 1.0 would not fully solve the problem, because the total net migration rate can still be negative after the boost when domestic out-migration dominates. An additive approach is the correct fix.

### 1.3 Secondary Issues

**Dead code**: The `migration_rates_pep_high.parquet` file (state total: +2,787 net migrants/year, vs baseline +1,485) is generated by the BEBR pipeline but never consumed. The `PEP_SCENARIO_FILE_MAP` in `run_pep_projections.py` exists but only verifies file existence.

**Wasted pre-engine adjustment**: `apply_scenario_rate_adjustments()` in `02_run_projections.py` applies `+15%` to constant PEP rates, but these are overridden by convergence rates inside the engine.

**Dual-adjustment architecture**: Scenario adjustments are applied in two places (pre-engine and in-engine), but only the in-engine path has effect when convergence rates are present.

## 3. Design Options

### Option A: Fixed Annual Increment (Additive Absolute)

**Concept**: Add a fixed number of additional net migrants per year, distributed across counties and age-sex-race cells using the Rogers-Castro migration pattern.

**Increment derivation**: The CBO January 2025 projected 2,010K national net immigration for 2025, versus a long-term baseline of ~1,080K. The "elevated" excess is ~930K nationally. At ND's 0.125% share, this implies ~1,166 additional people per year. Rounding to a round number: **+1,200 additional net migrants per year**.

Alternative derivation from ND PEP data: the 2023-2025 average international migration (+3,350) minus the 2015-2019 average (+1,642) = +1,709 excess. The +1,200 figure is conservative relative to this.

**Implementation**:
```yaml
high_growth:
  migration:
    type: "additive_boost"
    annual_increment: 1200  # additional net migrants/year
    distribution: "proportional_to_population"  # or "rogers_castro"
```

New code in `apply_migration_scenario()`:
```python
if isinstance(scenario, dict) and scenario.get("type") == "additive_boost":
    increment = scenario.get("annual_increment", 0)
    # Distribute increment across cohorts proportional to population
    # (requires population data -- architectural change needed)
```

**Pros**:
- Guarantees high_growth > baseline for all counties and all years (adding positive migrants always increases population)
- Directly models the concept: "more people arrive"
- Increment value is empirically grounded in CBO/PEP data
- Clear, simple semantics: baseline + X additional people

**Cons**:
- Requires population data in `apply_migration_scenario()`, which currently only receives migration rates -- significant architectural change
- Distribution method matters: proportional to population? proportional to recent international migration? uniform? Each choice has different county-level implications
- Convergence rates are expressed as rates (proportion of population), not absolute numbers. An additive absolute increment must be converted to a rate adjustment using current population, which varies by year
- The fixed increment does not decay over time. CBO projects the elevated level returning to baseline by ~2030, but the high_growth scenario assumes the counterfactual where it persists indefinitely. This is by design (it is a counterfactual), but it should be documented

### Option B: Factor-Based Additive via International Share (Recommended)

**Concept**: Mirror the restricted_growth mechanism in reverse. The restricted_growth scenario reduces international migration by CBO-derived factors. The high_growth scenario *increases* international migration by a CBO-derived factor, using the same `intl_share` decomposition.

**Key insight**: The `intl_share` mechanism decomposes total migration into domestic (unchanged) and international (scaled). For restricted_growth, factors < 1.0 reduce the international component. For high_growth, a factor > 1.0 increases it. The formula is the same:

```
effective_factor = 1 - intl_share * (1 - factor)
adjusted_rate = base_rate * effective_factor
```

With `intl_share = 0.91` and `factor = 1.82`:
```
effective_factor = 1 - 0.91 * (1 - 1.82) = 1 - 0.91 * (-0.82) = 1 + 0.746 = 1.746
```

Wait -- this still multiplies the total rate. If the total rate is negative, the result is more negative. **This does not solve the fundamental sign problem**.

However, the mathematical decomposition reveals why:

```
domestic_part = base_rate * (1 - intl_share)    # = base_rate * 0.09
intl_part     = base_rate * intl_share * factor  # = base_rate * 0.91 * factor
adjusted_rate = domestic_part + intl_part
```

For a county with base_rate = -0.01 (net out-migration):
- domestic_part = -0.01 * 0.09 = -0.0009
- intl_part = -0.01 * 0.91 * 1.82 = -0.01657

The international component is still negative because the total rate is negative. The decomposition does not "see" that the international component might actually be positive -- it applies the share proportionally.

**This option is rejected** for the same fundamental reason as the original `+15_percent`: multiplicative scaling of a signed net rate cannot guarantee directionality.

### Option C: International-Only Additive Boost (Recommended)

**Concept**: Add a fixed number of additional international migrants per year, distributed using the existing `intl_share` mechanism's conceptual framework but implemented as an additive term rather than a multiplicative factor.

**Increment derivation**:

Two independent methods converge on the same range:

1. **CBO-based (top-down)**: CBO Jan 2025 projected 2025 net immigration at 2,010K. Long-term baseline: ~1,080K. Excess: 930K. At ND's 0.125% share: **~1,163/year**.

2. **ND PEP-based (bottom-up)**: ND international migration 2023-2025 average (+3,350) minus 2000-2019 historical average (+865) = +2,486 excess. But the baseline projection already incorporates some of this surge through the convergence interpolation's recent-period weighting. The increment should represent the *additional* elevation that the high_growth counterfactual captures beyond what the baseline already embeds. A reasonable estimate is 30-50% of the excess: **~750-1,250/year**.

3. **BEBR high-vs-baseline (internal validation)**: The pre-computed `migration_rates_pep_high.parquet` already shows +1,302 more net migrants than baseline across all 53 counties, with the desirable property of being higher everywhere. This is an independent estimate from the BEBR trimmed-averaging methodology.

**Recommended increment: +1,300 additional net migrants per year**, grounded in the BEBR high-vs-baseline difference and bracketed by the CBO and PEP estimates.

**Implementation approach**:

Rather than requiring an absolute-to-rate conversion inside the engine (which needs population data), implement the additive boost as a **rate increment** computed at configuration time:

```
rate_increment_per_cell = annual_increment / state_population / n_cells_per_county
```

But this is fragile because population changes over time. A better approach: compute the increment as a fraction of the baseline convergence rates such that the net effect is approximately +1,300 additional migrants.

The cleanest implementation: **use the pre-computed high migration rates file as the source**, since it already contains the correct county-specific adjustments. This requires routing the convergence pipeline to produce scenario-specific outputs or applying the PEP high-vs-baseline differential as an additive correction to the convergence rates.

**Detailed implementation**:

```yaml
high_growth:
  migration:
    type: "additive_international"
    annual_increment: 1300  # additional net international migrants/year
    distribution: "rogers_castro"
```

In `apply_migration_scenario()`:
```python
if isinstance(scenario, dict) and scenario.get("type") == "additive_international":
    increment = scenario.get("annual_increment", 0)
    distribution = scenario.get("distribution", "rogers_castro")
    # Convert annual_increment to per-cell rate additions
    # using Rogers-Castro age weights and population proportions
    rate_addition = _compute_rate_addition(adjusted_rates, increment, distribution)
    adjusted_rates[migration_col] = adjusted_rates[migration_col] + rate_addition
```

**Pros**:
- Guarantees high_growth > baseline (adding a positive increment to every cell always increases population)
- Uses CBO-grounded increment value
- Preserves the existing rate-based architecture (rates, not absolutes)
- Additive semantics match the real-world process: additional immigrants arriving
- The Rogers-Castro distribution appropriately concentrates migrants in the 20-35 age peak
- Consistent with the restricted_growth mechanism: both model international migration changes, but in opposite directions

**Cons**:
- Converting an absolute increment to rate additions requires knowing the population for each cell, which changes each projection year. A static rate addition computed at projection start becomes less accurate as population diverges from the base year
- The Rogers-Castro distribution assumes international migrants follow the standard age pattern. In reality, refugee resettlement (a large component in ND) has a different age profile
- More complex than a simple string scenario like `"+15_percent"`

### Option D: Use Pre-Computed High Rates Directly

**Concept**: Route the engine to load `migration_rates_pep_high.parquet` instead of `migration_rates_pep_baseline.parquet` for the high_growth scenario, and generate scenario-specific convergence rates.

**Implementation**:

1. The convergence interpolation pipeline (`cohort_projections/data/process/convergence_interpolation.py`) already accepts per-county migration rates as input. Run it with the BEBR high scenario rates to produce `convergence_rates_by_year_high.parquet`

2. In `02_run_projections.py` `load_demographic_rates()`, accept a scenario parameter and load the appropriate convergence file

3. Remove the engine-level `+15_percent` application entirely -- the scenario difference is fully baked into the rates

**Pros**:
- Uses rates that are already computed and validated (all 53 counties higher than baseline)
- The BEBR high scenario represents the "most optimistic historical period" for each county -- a defensible empirical basis
- No engine code changes needed beyond file routing
- State-level difference (+1,302/year) is in the right range for a CBO-grounded high scenario
- County-level distribution is empirically determined by each county's own most-optimistic period, not by an assumed distribution pattern
- Convergence rates properly interpolate between recent and long-term, matching the baseline methodology

**Cons**:
- Requires running the convergence pipeline separately for the high scenario (pipeline complexity)
- The BEBR "high" = "most optimistic period" has a different conceptual basis than "CBO-elevated immigration." One is the best historical period (which for oil counties means 2010-2014 boom), the other is sustained international migration. These are different migration regimes
- The county-level distribution reflects historical patterns that may not match future international migration patterns
- Does not address the dual-adjustment architecture issue (pre-engine vs in-engine)

### Option E: Hybrid -- Additive International Boost via Time-Varying Dict

**Concept**: Extend the existing `time_varying` dict mechanism to support an `additive_increment` parameter alongside the existing multiplicative `schedule`. This is the most architecturally consistent approach because it reuses the infrastructure built for restricted_growth.

```yaml
high_growth:
  migration:
    type: "time_varying"
    schedule: {}  # No year-specific multiplicative factors
    default_factor: 1.00  # No multiplicative adjustment
    intl_share: 0.91
    additive_increment: 1300  # Additional international migrants/year
    distribution: "proportional"
```

In `apply_migration_scenario()`:
```python
if isinstance(scenario, dict) and scenario.get("type") == "time_varying":
    # Existing multiplicative logic (unchanged)
    factor = schedule.get(year, default_factor)
    if factor != 1.0:
        intl_share = scenario.get("intl_share", 1.0)
        effective_factor = 1.0 - intl_share * (1.0 - factor)
        adjusted_rates[migration_col] *= effective_factor

    # NEW: Additive increment
    additive = scenario.get("additive_increment", 0)
    if additive != 0:
        n_cells = len(adjusted_rates)
        # Distribute proportionally to existing |rate| or uniformly
        adjusted_rates[migration_col] += additive / n_cells  # simplified
```

**Pros**:
- Reuses the existing `time_varying` infrastructure
- Could be made time-varying in the future (e.g., higher increment in 2025-2029, lower after 2030)
- Clean separation: multiplicative for restricted_growth, additive for high_growth, both in the same mechanism
- Configuration-driven, easy to update

**Cons**:
- The `additive_increment` is in absolute migrants, but the convergence rates are per-cell rates. Naive division by n_cells ignores age/sex/race structure. Proper distribution requires population data or a Rogers-Castro pattern
- Mixing multiplicative and additive in the same dict is conceptually clean but implementation may be error-prone

## 4. Recommended Approach: Option D (Pre-Computed High Rates) with Option C Fallback

### Primary Recommendation: Option D

**Use the pre-computed BEBR high scenario rates** to generate high-growth convergence rates. This is the most defensible approach because:

1. **Already computed and validated**: The `migration_rates_pep_high.parquet` file exists, has +1,302 additional net migrants vs baseline, and is higher than baseline for all 53 counties

2. **Empirically grounded at the county level**: Each county's high rate is its own most-optimistic historical period from the BEBR multi-period averaging methodology. This is more defensible than distributing a state-level increment using assumed patterns

3. **No engine code changes**: The fix is in the pipeline routing, not the projection engine. The engine already supports scenario-specific rate files through the convergence mechanism

4. **The increment value (+1,302/year) is well-grounded**:
   - CBO-based top-down estimate: ~1,163/year
   - ND PEP-based excess: 750-1,250/year
   - BEBR high-vs-baseline: 1,302/year
   - All three methods converge on ~1,200-1,300/year

### Specific Increment Justification

| Derivation Method | Annual Increment | Source |
|-------------------|------------------|--------|
| CBO Jan 2025 elevated vs long-term, at ND share | ~1,163 | CBO 60875; PEP 2023-2025 |
| ND PEP international surge (50% of 2023-2025 excess over 2000-2019) | ~1,243 | PEP components |
| BEBR high-vs-baseline rate file difference | 1,302 | `migration_rates_pep_high.parquet` |
| **Recommended (BEBR-derived)** | **~1,300** | All three methods |

The +1,300 figure represents approximately 0.16% of the 2025 base population (799,358), which is a moderate and plausible migration increment.

### Fallback: Option C (Additive International Boost)

If generating separate convergence rate files for the high scenario proves too complex for the pipeline, implement Option C as a simpler alternative. This adds the increment as a rate correction inside the engine, distributed using the Rogers-Castro age pattern and proportional to county base populations.

## 6. Validation Criteria

The fix is correct if and only if all of the following hold:

### 6.1 Directional Correctness
- [ ] `high_growth` total population > `baseline` total population for every projected year (2026-2055)
- [ ] This holds for every county individually (53/53 counties)
- [ ] This holds for every 5-year age group at the state level

### 6.2 Magnitude Plausibility
- [ ] State-level population difference (high_growth - baseline) is between +500 and +5,000 per year in early years (2026-2030)
- [ ] Cumulative difference by 2055 is between +15,000 and +60,000 (reflecting ~1,300 additional migrants/year compounding over 30 years with births and mortality)
- [ ] No county shows a difference exceeding 50% of its baseline population by 2055

### 6.3 Scenario Ordering
- [ ] For all years: `restricted_growth` <= `baseline` <= `high_growth` (weak inequality; restricted may equal baseline after 2030 convergence)
- [ ] The three-scenario fan narrows over time as restricted_growth converges to baseline

### 6.4 Consistency with CBO
- [ ] The high-growth state-level excess is consistent with ND's ~0.125% share of the CBO January 2025 "elevated" projection
- [ ] The per-year migration increment is in the range of 1,000-2,000 additional net migrants (consistent with the CBO, PEP, and BEBR estimates)

### 6.5 No Regression
- [ ] Baseline scenario results are unchanged (same convergence rates, same engine logic)
- [ ] Restricted_growth scenario results are unchanged
- [ ] All existing unit tests pass
- [ ] Pre-commit checks pass (excluding pre-existing failures)

## 8. Open Questions

1. **Should the high_growth scenario be time-varying?** The CBO January 2025 "elevated" level was projected to decline from 2,010K (2025) to ~1,080K (2030+). Should the high_growth scenario also decay, or is a constant increment the correct counterfactual ("what if elevation persisted indefinitely")?

2. **BEBR high vs CBO-grounded high**: The BEBR high scenario uses the "most optimistic historical period" for each county, which for oil counties reflects 2010-2014 boom-era domestic migration, not international migration. Should the increment be restricted to international migration channels only (matching the CBO grounding), or should it also capture optimistic domestic migration?

3. **Interaction with boom dampening**: If using BEBR high rates, the boom-dampened counties (Williams, McKenzie, etc.) will have their most-optimistic period reflect dampened boom rates. Should the high scenario use undampened or dampened rates?

4. **Fertility interaction**: The current `+5_percent` fertility adjustment is retained. Is this still appropriate under an additive migration approach, or should it be recalibrated?
